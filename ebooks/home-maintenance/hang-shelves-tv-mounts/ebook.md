<a name="introduction"></a>
### Introduction
One of the most rewarding ways to make a house feel like a home is to put things on the walls. Whether it's a gallery of family photos, a set of functional shelves, or a sleek wall-mounted TV, taking things off the floor opens up your space and adds personality.

However, many people (especially those living solo for the first time) are terrified of "ruining the walls" or, worse, having a heavy shelf crash down in the middle of the night. This guide is here to take away that fear. You will learn how to identify your wall type, choose the right hardware, and mount anything securely—even if you've never picked up a drill before.

<a name="wall-anatomy"></a>
### Wall Anatomy: What's Behind the Paint?
Before you hammer a single nail, you need to know what you're hitting.
- **Drywall (Gypsum Board):** The most common in modern homes. It’s essentially compressed chalk between paper. It's soft and cannot hold weight on its own.
- **Plaster:** Found in older homes. It's harder and more brittle than drywall, often backed by wooden strips (lath).
- **Masonry (Brick/Concrete):** Found in basements, industrial lofts, or exterior walls. Requires a special drill bit.
- **Studs:** The wooden (or sometimes metal) vertical "ribs" of your house. They are usually 16 or 24 inches apart. **Mounting to a stud is always the gold standard for strength.**

<a name="stud-finding"></a>
### Chapter 1: The Art of Stud Finding
If you are hanging anything over 10 lbs (like a large mirror or TV), you really should find a stud.
1. **The Electronic Way:** Slide your stud finder horizontally across the wall. When it beeps or lights up, mark both edges of the stud to find the center.
2. **The Magnetic Way:** Slide a strong magnet in an "S" pattern. It will "stick" to the wall when it finds a drywall screw—which is always driven into a stud.
3. **The Knock Test:** Knock on the wall. A hollow sound means "no stud." A solid, dull thud means you've found one.

<a name="anchors"></a>
### Chapter 2: Anchors Away! Mounting Without a Stud
What if there's no stud where you want your shelf? That's where anchors come in.

<a name="drywall-anchors"></a>
#### Drywall Anchors
- **Plastic Expansion Anchors:** For very light things (small photos).
- **Self-Drilling (Threaded) Anchors:** These look like big plastic screws. They are great for medium weight (up to 20-30 lbs) and don't require a pilot hole.
- **Toggle Bolts:** The "heavy hitters." They have metal "wings" that open behind the drywall. These can hold 50+ lbs easily. Use these for floating shelves if you can't hit a stud.

<a name="masonry-tile"></a>
#### Masonry & Tile Mounting
- **Masonry:** You need a "Masonry Bit" (it has a little "hat" on the tip) and plastic sleeves or "Tapcon" screws.
- **Tile:** Use a "Diamond" or "Carbide-tipped" bit. **Pro Tip:** Put a piece of painter's tape over the tile where you want to drill to keep the bit from sliding around and scratching the surface.

<a name="leveling"></a>
### Chapter 3: Perfect Leveling & Layout
Crooked pictures are the mark of an amateur.
1. **The Painter's Tape Trick:** If an item has two hanging holes on the back, put a piece of tape across the holes and mark them on the tape. Peel the tape off, put it on the wall, level it, and drill through your marks.
2. **The "Eyeline" Rule:** Most art should be hung so the center is 57 to 60 inches from the floor (average human eye level).

### Chapter 4: Mounting specific items

<a name="tv-mounts"></a>
#### TV Wall Mounts
1. **Find Two Studs:** TV mounts *must* be screwed into studs.
2. **Height:** Sit on your couch. Your eyes should be level with the bottom third of the TV screen. Don't hang it too high (the "r/TVTooHigh" mistake)!
3. **Mount the Bracket:** Screw the wall plate into the studs using the provided lag bolts. Then attach the arms to the back of the TV and lift it onto the plate.

<a name="patching"></a>
### Chapter 5: Patching Mistakes
Drilled a hole in the wrong spot? Don't panic.
1. **Small Holes:** Put a tiny dab of white spackle on your finger and rub it into the hole. Wipe flush with a damp rag.
2. **Medium Holes:** Use a putty knife to apply spackle. Let dry, sand lightly, and dab with a bit of matching wall paint using a cotton swab.

<a name="conclusion"></a>
### Conclusion
Your walls are a canvas, not something to be feared. By understanding anchors and studs, you have the power to transform your living space. Start small with a single photo, gain confidence, and soon you'll be mounting TVs and built-in shelves like a seasoned pro. You've got this!
